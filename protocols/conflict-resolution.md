# Conflict Resolution Protocol (Integrated)

## Prerequisites
- Both partners willing to engage (if one refuses, use the One-Partner Protocol below)
- No active safety concerns (abuse, threats, substance impairment)
- Basic agreement: "We want to resolve this, not win"

## Phase 1: PAUSE - Physiological Regulation (5-20 minutes)

### Flooding Check
Signs of physiological flooding (DPA - Diffuse Physiological Arousal):
- Heart rate above 100 BPM
- Shallow breathing or breath-holding
- Muscle tension (jaw, fists, shoulders)
- Tunnel vision, inability to hear partner's words
- Feeling of "I can't take this anymore"

### If Flooded
1. Call a structured timeout: "I'm flooding. I need [20 minutes/an hour] to calm down. I WILL come back to this conversation at [specific time]."
2. During timeout: Walk, breathe, listen to music. Do NOT rehearse arguments or build your case.
3. Self-soothing techniques:
   - 4-7-8 breathing (inhale 4 sec, hold 7, exhale 8)
   - Progressive muscle relaxation
   - Grounding: name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you touch
4. Return at agreed time. If still flooded, request extension with new specific time.

### Anti-Pattern
NEVER storm out without a return time. "I'm leaving" without a commitment to return activates abandonment fears and escalates the conflict.

## Phase 2: LISTEN - Structured Dialogue (15-30 minutes)

### Speaker Rules
- Use NVC format: Observation -> Feeling -> Need -> Request
- Speak for yourself only ("I feel..." not "You make me feel...")
- Stay with ONE issue at a time (no kitchen-sinking)
- Keep statements to 2-3 sentences before pausing for listener

### Listener Rules
- Full attention (phone down, face partner, eye contact)
- No interrupting, no preparing your rebuttal
- Paraphrase after speaker finishes: "What I hear you saying is..."
- Ask: "Did I get that right? Is there more?"
- Validate (validation =/= agreement): "That makes sense that you'd feel that way"

### Switch
After Speaker A feels fully heard and Listener B has demonstrated understanding, switch roles.

### Key Principle
The goal of this phase is UNDERSTANDING, not AGREEMENT. You don't have to agree with your partner to validate their experience.

## Phase 3: ANALYZE - Pattern Recognition (10-15 minutes)

Together, examine the conflict through multiple lenses:

### Gottman Check
- Were any of the Four Horsemen present? Which ones?
- What antidotes could have been used?
- Were repair attempts made? Were they accepted or rejected?

### Attachment Lens
- Which attachment system was activated?
- "When [event] happened, I interpreted it as [meaning], which activated my fear of [abandonment/engulfment]"
- "What I actually needed was [attachment need: reassurance / space / presence / acknowledgment]"

### CBT Lens
- Were cognitive distortions at play? (mind reading, catastrophizing, all-or-nothing)
- "The story I told myself was... The evidence for and against that story is..."

### Psychoanalytic Lens (optional, for deeper patterns)
- "This reminded me of... from my past"
- "I might be projecting my [quality] onto you"
- "The intensity of my reaction suggests an older wound is activated"

## Phase 4: BRIDGE - Solution Building (10-15 minutes)

### Map the Circular Dynamic
"We get caught in a cycle where I [behavior], which leads you to [behavior], which leads me to [behavior]..."

### Gender-Aware Translation
If applicable, translate needs across communication styles:
- "When you say 'I need space,' what I hear is rejection. What you actually mean is..."
- "When I say 'we need to talk,' what you hear is criticism. What I actually mean is..."

### Actionable Commitments
Each partner identifies ONE small, specific, behavioral change:
- NOT: "I'll be more supportive" (vague)
- YES: "When you come home stressed, I'll ask 'Do you want me to listen or help solve?' before offering advice" (specific, behavioral)

### The 2% Rule
Find the 2% of your partner's position you can honestly agree with. Acknowledge it explicitly: "You're right that I could have..."

## Phase 5: REPAIR - Reconnection (5-10 minutes)

### Repair Attempt Menu
Choose at least one:
- [ ] Genuine apology: "I'm sorry for [specific behavior]. I know it [specific impact]."
- [ ] Acknowledgment: "I see how my [action] hurt you, and that wasn't my intention."
- [ ] Physical reconnection: Hold hands, hug (if mutually welcome)
- [ ] Humor: (Only if organic and not dismissive)
- [ ] Appreciation: "Thank you for staying in this conversation with me."
- [ ] Future commitment: "Next time this comes up, I want to try [new approach]."
- [ ] Shared activity: Do something enjoyable together afterward

### Schedule Follow-Up
Set a specific check-in: "Let's revisit this on [day] to see how we're both feeling."

## One-Partner Protocol

When only one partner is willing to engage:

1. Do not force the reluctant partner. Coerced participation backfires.
2. Model the behavior you want to see:
   - Use NVC even if your partner doesn't
   - Take responsibility for your part even if your partner doesn't reciprocate
   - Make repair attempts even if they're not returned
3. Focus on what you can control: your own responses, your own patterns
4. Set boundaries with compassion: "I need [X]. I understand you need time. I'm willing to wait until [reasonable timeframe]."
5. If the pattern persists, consider individual therapy and/or professional couple's counseling.

## Emergency De-Escalation

When conflict is actively escalating and the above protocol can't be initiated:

1. STOP talking about the content of the argument
2. Name what's happening: "We're escalating. Neither of us is going to hear the other right now."
3. Physical state change: Stand up, get water, move to a different room
4. Use the simplest possible repair: "I love you and I don't want to hurt you."
5. Agree to revisit after a specific cooling period
6. If one partner continues to escalate after the other requests a pause, the requesting partner should calmly leave the room (not the house) and return after self-soothing
