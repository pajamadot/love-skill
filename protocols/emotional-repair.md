# Emotional Repair Protocol

## When to Use
- Trust has been broken (betrayal, infidelity, broken promises)
- Harsh words were said during conflict that caused lasting hurt
- One partner feels chronically unheard, unseen, or undervalued
- An event caused emotional injury that hasn't healed despite time passing

## The Anatomy of Emotional Injury

Not all hurts are equal. Assess the nature and depth:

### Surface Injury (Days to heal)
- Forgetting an important date
- Saying something thoughtless
- Being distracted during important conversation
- Treatment: Simple acknowledgment + brief apology + behavioral adjustment

### Moderate Injury (Weeks to heal)
- Breaking a promise
- Dismissing partner's feelings during vulnerable moment
- Repeated small hurts that accumulate
- Treatment: Full repair protocol below

### Deep Injury (Months to heal)
- Infidelity (emotional or physical)
- Betrayal of confidence (sharing private information)
- Gaslighting or manipulation
- Abandonment during crisis (medical emergency, loss, etc.)
- Treatment: Full repair protocol + professional support recommended

## The Seven-Step Repair Process

### Step 1: ACKNOWLEDGE - Name What Happened
The injuring partner describes what they did in specific, non-minimizing language.

**Anti-patterns:**
- "If I hurt you..." (conditional - implies it might not have happened)
- "I'm sorry you feel that way" (places ownership on the hurt partner)
- "It wasn't that bad" (minimizing)
- "But you also..." (deflecting with counter-accusations)

**Model:**
"I [specific action]. I understand that this [specific impact on you]. This was [wrong / hurtful / a betrayal of your trust]."

### Step 2: VALIDATE - Honor the Pain
Acknowledge that the hurt partner's reaction is legitimate.

"Your pain makes sense. If someone I trusted did this to me, I would feel [same emotion] too. You have every right to feel [their stated emotion]."

**Key**: Validation does NOT mean agreeing with everything the hurt partner says. It means acknowledging their EXPERIENCE as real and legitimate.

### Step 3: TAKE RESPONSIBILITY - Own Without Excuses
Accept full responsibility for the behavior (not for the hurt partner's feelings, but for the actions that caused them).

"I chose to [action]. No one made me do that. I take full responsibility for that choice and its consequences."

**Do not explain "why" unless asked.** Explanations at this stage are often experienced as excuses. The hurt partner will ask for context when they're ready.

### Step 4: EXPRESS GENUINE REMORSE
Empathy-based remorse, not guilt-based self-flagellation.

"I feel terrible knowing that I caused you this pain. I can see how my actions affected your sense of [safety / trust / self-worth / etc.]. I wish I had made a different choice."

**Red flag**: If the injuring partner's remorse centers on THEIR OWN suffering ("I feel so awful about myself"), redirect to the impact on the other: "This is about what your partner experienced, not about your guilt."

### Step 5: MAKE AMENDS - Specific Behavioral Commitments
Vague promises ("I'll be better") are meaningless. Specific behavioral commitments are the currency of trust repair.

"Going forward, I commit to:
1. [Specific action] when [specific situation]
2. [Specific transparency measure] to rebuild trust
3. [Specific boundary I will maintain]"

**Example (after emotional infidelity):**
"I will end all private communication with that person. I will be transparent about my phone and social media. I will check in with you about my whereabouts without you having to ask."

### Step 6: REQUEST PATIENCE
Acknowledge that healing is not linear and the hurt partner sets the pace.

"I understand that trust takes time to rebuild. I won't pressure you to 'move on' or 'get over it.' You can bring this up again when you need to, and I will listen without defensiveness."

**Critical**: The injuring partner must tolerate the hurt partner's need to re-process the event. Saying "we already talked about this" after a major injury is re-injuring.

### Step 7: REBUILD - Consistent Trust-Building Over Time
Trust is rebuilt through many small actions, not one grand gesture.

**Trust Account Deposits:**
- Doing what you say you'll do (reliability)
- Being transparent without being asked (proactive honesty)
- Showing up emotionally (presence)
- Remembering and honoring the impact of what happened (acknowledgment)
- Changing the behavior that caused harm (evidence of growth)

**Timeline Expectations:**
- Surface injury: 1-2 weeks of consistent behavior
- Moderate injury: 1-3 months
- Deep injury: 6-18 months (with professional support)
- Full trust may never return to pre-injury levels, but a NEW, deeper trust can develop

## The Hurt Partner's Role

The hurt partner is NOT required to:
- Forgive on any timeline
- "Get over it" quickly
- Pretend things are normal
- Trust before trust is earned

The hurt partner IS asked to:
- Give the injuring partner the opportunity to repair (if they choose to stay)
- Communicate what they need for healing (not leave the partner guessing)
- Notice and acknowledge genuine repair efforts (even if they're not yet "enough")
- Differentiate between healthy processing and punitive behavior

## When Repair Fails

Signs that professional help is needed:
- Repair attempts are consistently rejected over weeks/months
- The injuring partner repeatedly breaks new commitments
- The hurt partner cannot stop punishing (the relationship has become toxic)
- Either partner feels unsafe (physically or emotionally)
- The couple cannot discuss the event without re-traumatizing

**Recommendation:** Individual therapy for both partners PLUS couple's therapy with a licensed professional specializing in betrayal recovery (e.g., Gottman-trained therapist, EFT practitioner).
