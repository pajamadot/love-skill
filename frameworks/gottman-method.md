# Gottman Method Framework

## The Sound Relationship House (SRH)

John Gottman's research-based model for relationship health, built from bottom up:

### Level 1: Love Maps
- How well do you know your partner's inner world?
- Their dreams, fears, favorite things, current stresses
- Exercise: "Love Map" questionnaire - 20 questions about your partner's inner life

### Level 2: Fondness and Admiration System
- The antidote to contempt
- Can you identify qualities you admire in your partner?
- Exercise: List 3 things you appreciate about your partner daily
- The 5:1 ratio: 5 positive interactions for every 1 negative

### Level 3: Turning Toward
- Responding to partner's "bids" for emotional connection
- A bid can be: a question, a look, a touch, a sigh, sharing something funny
- Masters turn toward 86% of the time; disasters turn toward 33%
- Exercise: Track bids and responses for one day

### Level 4: Positive Sentiment Override (PSO)
- When PSO is active: partner's ambiguous behavior is interpreted positively
- When Negative Sentiment Override (NSO) is active: even positive gestures are interpreted as hostile
- PSO is built through consistent attention to Levels 1-3

### Level 5: Manage Conflict
- 69% of relationship problems are perpetual (unsolvable)
- Perpetual problems need dialogue, not solution
- Solvable problems need compromise protocol:
  1. Soften startup
  2. Accept influence
  3. Repair and de-escalate
  4. Compromise

### Level 6: Make Life Dreams Come True
- Support each other's aspirations and sense of purpose
- Explore the "dream within the conflict" - often fights about money are really about security vs. freedom

### Level 7: Create Shared Meaning
- Rituals of connection (weekly date, morning coffee together)
- Shared goals and values
- A sense of "we-ness" vs. "you vs. me"

## The Four Horsemen - Detailed

### Criticism -> Antidote: Gentle Startup
**Pattern**: "You never help around the house. You're so lazy."
**Antidote**: "I'm feeling overwhelmed with housework. Could we talk about how to share it more?"
**Formula**: I feel [emotion] about [specific situation]. I need [specific request].

### Contempt -> Antidote: Culture of Appreciation
**Pattern**: Eye-rolling, sarcasm, mockery, "You're pathetic"
**Antidote**: Daily appreciation ("Thank you for making dinner"), fondness rituals
**Key insight**: Contempt is the #1 predictor of divorce. It communicates disgust and superiority.

### Defensiveness -> Antidote: Accept Responsibility
**Pattern**: "It's not my fault! You're the one who..." or playing the victim
**Antidote**: "You have a point. I could have handled that differently."
**Key insight**: Even accepting 2% responsibility defuses the cycle.

### Stonewalling -> Antidote: Physiological Self-Soothing
**Pattern**: Shutting down, turning away, going silent, "flooding"
**Antidote**: "I'm feeling overwhelmed. I need 20 minutes to calm down, and then I'll come back to this conversation."
**Key insight**: Stonewalling occurs when heart rate exceeds 100 BPM (Diffuse Physiological Arousal). The person literally cannot process information. A break is physiologically necessary, not avoidance.

## Repair Attempts

The #1 predictor of relationship success is not the absence of conflict, but the ability to repair after conflict.

**Types of repair attempts:**
- Humor: "Okay, I think we've officially gone crazy. Can we start over?"
- Affection: Physical touch during argument (if welcome)
- Meta-communication: "I realize I'm getting defensive. Let me try again."
- Responsibility: "My part in this is..."
- Appreciation: "I know this is hard for both of us, and I appreciate you staying in this conversation"
- Common ground: "We both want the same thing - we just disagree on how to get there"

## Dreams Within Conflict

When perpetual conflicts feel gridlocked, explore the deeper meaning:

Questions to uncover the dream:
1. "What does this issue mean to you?"
2. "Is there a story behind this for you?"
3. "What would it mean if you got what you wanted here?"
4. "What are you afraid would happen if you compromised?"
5. "Does this connect to something from your childhood or past?"
