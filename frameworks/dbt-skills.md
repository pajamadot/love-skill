# Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) Skills for Couples - Marsha Linehan

## Core Principle
DBT provides concrete, teachable skills for managing intense emotions in relationships. The "dialectical" stance holds that two seemingly opposite things can both be true: "I love you AND I'm furious with you." This both/and thinking prevents the all-or-nothing traps that destroy relationships.

## The Four Skill Modules

### 1. Mindfulness - Observing Without Reacting

**What Skills** (What to do):
- **Observe**: Notice what's happening inside you without acting on it. "I notice my chest tightening. I notice the thought 'they don't care.'"
- **Describe**: Put words to your experience without judgment. "I'm feeling hurt" not "You're being cruel."
- **Participate**: Fully engage in the present moment rather than mentally rehearsing your rebuttal.

**How Skills** (How to do it):
- **Non-Judgmentally**: Replace "You SHOULD have..." with "I preferred..." Replace "That was WRONG" with "That didn't work for me."
- **One-Mindfully**: Stay in THIS conversation, THIS moment. Don't bring in last week's fight.
- **Effectively**: Do what WORKS, not what feels "right." Ask: "Will this response bring us closer or push us apart?"

**Couple Application:**
Before responding in conflict, take 3 breaths and practice the STOP skill:
- **S**top what you're doing
- **T**ake a step back
- **O**bserve what's happening (body, thoughts, emotions)
- **P**roceed mindfully (choose response rather than react)

### 2. Distress Tolerance - Surviving Crisis Without Making It Worse

**TIPP Skills** (Change your body chemistry in 30 seconds):
- **T**emperature: Splash cold water on face or hold ice cubes. Activates dive reflex, slows heart rate.
- **I**ntense exercise: 20 jumping jacks, sprint up stairs. Burns off adrenaline.
- **P**aced breathing: Exhale longer than inhale (4-in, 7-out). Activates parasympathetic system.
- **P**rogressive muscle relaxation: Tense-release each muscle group.

**ACCEPTS** (Distract until the crisis passes):
- **A**ctivities: Do something engaging (not phone-scrolling)
- **C**ontributing: Help someone else (shifts focus outward)
- **C**omparisons: "We've survived worse than this"
- **E**motions: Watch something funny, listen to calming music
- **P**ushing away: Mentally shelve the issue for 20 minutes
- **T**houghts: Count backward from 100, name states alphabetically
- **S**ensations: Hold ice, smell something strong - ground in body

**Radical Acceptance:**
"This is what's happening right now. Fighting reality doesn't change it - it only adds suffering."
- NOT approval or endorsement
- NOT giving up
- IS acknowledging what IS so you can respond effectively
- "I accept that my partner said something hurtful. Now I can choose how to respond."

**Couple Application:**
When a fight is escalating beyond either partner's window of tolerance:
1. Use TIPP to physiologically regulate (both partners)
2. Use Radical Acceptance: "We're both activated right now. This is not the time to solve this."
3. Set a return time: "Let's revisit this at 8pm when we're both calmer."

### 3. Emotion Regulation - Understanding and Managing Emotional Intensity

**Check the Facts:**
When emotional intensity seems disproportionate to the situation:
1. What is the EMOTION I'm experiencing?
2. What is the EVENT that triggered it?
3. What am I INTERPRETING or ASSUMING about the event?
4. Does my interpretation fit the FACTS?
5. Am I assuming THREAT where there may be none?
6. Is this a 10/10 situation or am I AMPLIFYING?

**Opposite Action:**
When your emotion doesn't fit the facts OR acting on it will make things worse:

| Emotion | Urge | Opposite Action |
|---|---|---|
| Anger (unjustified) | Attack, criticize | Gently approach, empathize |
| Fear (unjustified) | Avoid, withdraw | Approach what you fear (the conversation) |
| Shame (unjustified) | Hide, disappear | Share with partner (appropriate vulnerability) |
| Sadness (prolonged) | Isolate, shut down | Engage, activate, connect |
| Guilt (unjustified) | Over-apologize, self-punish | Let it go, accept imperfection |

**Build Mastery:**
Do one thing each day that builds competence. In relationship context:
- Successfully repair one small conflict
- Express one need clearly
- Receive one piece of feedback without defending

**Couple Application:**
Teach both partners to use "Check the Facts" before escalating:
"Before I respond, let me check: Is my partner actually attacking me, or am I interpreting their stress as rejection?"

### 4. Interpersonal Effectiveness - Getting What You Need While Keeping the Relationship

**DEAR MAN** (Getting what you want):
- **D**escribe the situation objectively (facts only)
- **E**xpress your feelings using "I" statements
- **A**ssert your need clearly (specific request)
- **R**einforce by explaining the positive outcome ("If we do this, we'll both feel...")
- **M**indful - stay on topic, don't get pulled into side arguments
- **A**ppear confident (even if you don't feel it)
- **N**egotiate - be willing to give to get

**GIVE** (Keeping the relationship):
- **G**entle - no attacks, threats, or judgments
- **I**nterested - listen, ask questions, lean in
- **V**alidate - acknowledge partner's perspective as legitimate
- **E**asy manner - use humor, lightness (not sarcasm)

**FAST** (Keeping your self-respect):
- **F**air - be fair to both yourself AND your partner
- **A**pologies (don't over-apologize) - only apologize for what you actually did wrong
- **S**tick to values - don't agree to things that violate your principles
- **T**ruthful - don't exaggerate, minimize, or lie

**Couple Application:**
For difficult conversations, prepare using all three:
"I want to ask for [DEAR MAN] in a way that preserves our relationship [GIVE] without abandoning my own needs [FAST]."

## Dialectical Thinking in Relationships

### Core Dialectics for Couples

| Thesis | Antithesis | Synthesis |
|---|---|---|
| "I need closeness" | "I need space" | "We can be close AND have individual space" |
| "I'm right about this" | "You're right about this" | "We each hold part of the truth" |
| "I need to change you" | "I need to accept you" | "I accept you AND I can ask for change" |
| "This is unbearable" | "I can handle anything" | "This is very hard AND I can cope with it" |
| "I should leave" | "I should stay no matter what" | "I choose to stay AND I have limits" |

### Walking the Middle Path
- Avoid extremes ("always/never," "perfect/terrible")
- Validate AND push for change simultaneously
- Accept what is AND work toward what could be
- Be gentle with yourself AND hold yourself accountable

## Window of Tolerance (Integration with Polyvagal Theory)

```
        HYPERAROUSAL (Fight/Flight)
        - Anger, anxiety, panic
        - Racing thoughts, yelling
        - Inability to listen
        ─────────────────────────────
        WINDOW OF TOLERANCE
        - Can think and feel simultaneously
        - Can listen to partner
        - Can problem-solve
        - Productive conflict happens HERE
        ─────────────────────────────
        HYPOAROUSAL (Freeze/Shutdown)
        - Numbness, dissociation
        - Stonewalling, blank stare
        - "I don't care anymore"
```

**Goal**: Keep both partners within their window of tolerance during conflict.
**If either partner leaves the window**: STOP the content discussion, regulate first, return when both are back inside.
