# Nonviolent Communication (NVC) Framework - Marshall Rosenberg

## Core Philosophy
All human behavior is an attempt to meet universal human needs. Conflict arises not from the needs themselves (which are universal) but from the strategies we use to meet those needs.

## The Four Components

### 1. Observation (Without Evaluation)

**Principle**: Describe what happened factually, as a camera would record it.

| Evaluation (Avoid) | Observation (Use) |
|---|---|
| "You're always on your phone" | "I noticed you checked your phone 6 times during dinner" |
| "You don't care about our plans" | "You arrived 30 minutes after the agreed time" |
| "You're being cold" | "You haven't initiated physical affection in the past week" |
| "You snapped at me" | "When I asked about the bills, your voice was raised and you said 'not now'" |

**Why it matters**: Evaluations trigger defensiveness. Observations invite curiosity.

### 2. Feelings (Not Thoughts Disguised as Feelings)

**Principle**: Express what you FEEL, not what you THINK the other person did to you.

| Pseudo-Feelings (Thoughts) | Real Feelings |
|---|---|
| "I feel abandoned" | "I feel lonely and scared" |
| "I feel manipulated" | "I feel confused and frustrated" |
| "I feel unappreciated" | "I feel sad and discouraged" |
| "I feel attacked" | "I feel hurt and defensive" |
| "I feel ignored" | "I feel lonely and invisible" |

**Feelings Vocabulary - Expanded:**

When needs ARE met: alive, amazed, appreciative, calm, comfortable, confident, curious, delighted, eager, energetic, fulfilled, glad, grateful, happy, hopeful, inspired, joyful, loving, moved, optimistic, peaceful, playful, proud, refreshed, relieved, satisfied, secure, tender, thrilled, touched, trusting, warm

When needs are NOT met: afraid, angry, annoyed, anxious, bored, confused, dejected, disappointed, discouraged, distressed, embarrassed, exhausted, frustrated, guilty, helpless, hopeless, hurt, impatient, irritated, jealous, lonely, nervous, overwhelmed, puzzled, regretful, resentful, restless, sad, scared, shocked, suspicious, tense, tired, uncomfortable, uneasy, unhappy, vulnerable, withdrawn, worried

### 3. Needs (Universal Human Needs)

**Principle**: Behind every feeling is a need. Needs are universal; strategies are personal.

**Comprehensive Needs Inventory:**

**Connection**: acceptance, affection, appreciation, belonging, closeness, community, companionship, consideration, empathy, inclusion, intimacy, love, mutuality, respect, safety, security, support, to know and be known, to see and be seen, trust, understanding, warmth

**Autonomy**: choice, freedom, independence, space, spontaneity, self-determination

**Meaning**: awareness, celebration, challenge, clarity, competence, consciousness, contribution, creativity, discovery, efficacy, effectiveness, growth, hope, learning, mourning, participation, purpose, self-expression, stimulation, understanding

**Physical Well-being**: air, food, movement, rest, sexual expression, safety, shelter, touch, water

**Honesty**: authenticity, integrity, presence, transparency

**Peace**: beauty, communion, ease, equality, harmony, inspiration, order

**Play**: humor, joy, fun, laughter, relaxation, rejuvenation

### 4. Request (Not Demand)

**Principle**: A request is specific, actionable, and allows the other person to say no.

| Demand (Avoid) | Request (Use) |
|---|---|
| "Stop being on your phone at dinner" | "Would you be willing to put your phone in another room during dinner?" |
| "You need to be more romantic" | "Would you be willing to plan one date for us this month?" |
| "Don't talk to me like that" | "Would you be willing to lower your voice and repeat what you said?" |
| "You have to change" | "Would you be willing to try [specific thing] for one week?" |

**How to know if it's a request or demand**: If the other person says "no" and you punish, withdraw, or guilt-trip, it was a demand.

## NVC in Practice: Complete Examples

### Example 1: Feeling Neglected
**Before NVC**: "You never spend time with me anymore. All you care about is work."
**After NVC**: "When I notice we haven't had an uninterrupted conversation in two weeks [observation], I feel lonely and disconnected [feelings], because I need closeness and quality time with you [needs]. Would you be willing to set aside one evening this week where we turn off our devices and just talk? [request]"

### Example 2: Feeling Criticized
**Before NVC**: "Stop nagging me about the house. Nothing I do is ever good enough for you."
**After NVC**: "When I hear comments about what I haven't cleaned [observation], I feel discouraged and defensive [feelings], because I need appreciation for what I do contribute and to feel competent [needs]. Would you be willing to acknowledge what I have done before mentioning what's left? [request]"

### Example 3: Empathic Reception
When your partner says something critical, receive it through NVC:

Partner says: "You're so irresponsible with money!"
NVC translation (in your mind): "My partner is feeling anxious [feeling] because they need financial security [need]. Their strategy for expressing this is criticism, but the need underneath is valid."
NVC response: "It sounds like you're worried about our finances [empathic guess]. Is financial security what you need right now? [need check] Can we sit down together this weekend and look at our budget? [offer]"

## Self-Empathy: The Hidden Foundation

Before you can speak NVC to your partner, practice it with yourself:

1. Notice your trigger: "They just said X and I feel furious"
2. Self-empathy: "I feel furious because I need respect and consideration"
3. Choose: "Now that I understand my own need, how do I want to express it?"

Self-empathy prevents reactive communication and creates space for conscious choice.
